Group3 HW #1.1 — To-Do

UX evaluation of two government cultural websites through scenario-based user interviews.

🌐 Taiwan World Heritage Potential Sites 🏛 Ministry of Culture — Who We Are
Scenarios2
Scenario 1
✈️
Foreign tourist planning a 3-day cultural trip
Browse the MOC English website for an overview of Taiwan's cultural heritage, then find a site of interest and evaluate if the information is sufficient to plan a travel itinerary.
Scenario 2
📚
Student researching Taiwan's Cultural Preservation Policies
Find historical background of a specific site on the Heritage website, then search for relevant preservation laws on the MOC website to support academic research.
Interview Questions10 questions
Phase 1 — Warm-up & Behavioral Context
Q1When planning a deep cultural trip or looking for academic data, do you usually start with a search engine or go directly to an official government website? Why?
Q2What are the most common frustrations you've experienced when using official government or cultural websites?
Phase 2 — Initial Visual & Brand Impression
Q3Does the overall visual style feel more like an "engaging travel brochure" or a "formal government archive"?
Q4Is it immediately clear where to find "attractions" versus "laws and regulations"?
Phase 3 — Feedback for Scenario 1 (Travel Planning)
Q5Which elements (photos, historical stories, maps) were most effective in making you want to visit a site in person?
Q6Do you feel the information is complete enough to plan a travel itinerary? What's missing?
Phase 4 — Feedback for Scenario 2 (Academic Search)
Q7When switching between the two sites, did it feel seamless or like two completely unrelated systems?
Q8Do you find the MOC English site content easy to understand, or does it feel filled with "officialese"?
Phase 5 — Wrap-up & Future Improvements
Q9If the websites had an AI assistant to summarize content or recommend routes, how helpful would that be?
Q10If you could change one thing, would it be the "interface design" or the "utility of the content"?

Key Insights
NavigationInformation is buried and hard to discover
Both participants struggled to find relevant content on the MOC website. Key information like exhibitions and cultural sites is hidden deep within nested navigation menus, requiring significant effort to locate.
「就只有那些詞，所以都是好理解的，只是會有點難找到想要的東西。」
— Participant 1
「它非常的多分頁，那就會很難找到。」
— Participant 2
ContentLack of practical travel information prevents itinerary planning
Neither website provides actionable details such as opening hours, ticket prices, GPS coordinates, or transportation routes. Both participants said they would need additional Google searches before planning a real trip.
「幾乎每個你想要把它排進行程都需要再做額外的搜尋，例如說哪一些東西、例如營業時間位置之類的。」
— Participant 1
「從它的官方網站上沒有辦法看出它的距離或是路線這樣子。」
— Participant 2
LocalizationChinese-only content breaks the foreign-user experience
Despite having an English-language version, both websites contain significant amounts of Chinese text — especially in exhibition posters and scrolling banners — making them inaccessible to international users.
「它的展覽的海報全部都是中文。」
— Participant 2
「如果英文版或是外國版的海報會更好一點。」
— Participant 1
Visual DesignImages are the primary motivator — but not enough on their own
Both participants cited photos as the most compelling element when browsing heritage sites. However, images alone are insufficient without supporting English text and practical logistics information.
「照片吧，就是上面有一些圖片吸引你，覺得這個東西看起來好像蠻漂亮的。」
— Participant 1
OpportunityStrong demand for AI-powered assistance
Both participants responded enthusiastically to the idea of an AI assistant — highlighting its potential to overcome language barriers, summarize dense content, and generate personalized travel routes.
「那一定要的，太有幫助了！如果旁邊能匯入一個 AI 助手直接用問他的方式，我就不用透過 Google 和網站。」
— Participant 1
「跟 AI 溝通會比較方便，然後語言上也比較不會有疙瘩。」
— Participant 2
Summary
Both websites are perceived as formal government archives rather than engaging cultural platforms. Users default to Google because official sites lack the discoverability and actionable content needed for real-world planning. The strongest design opportunities lie in integrating practical travel logistics, improving English localization, and introducing AI-assisted navigation to bridge the gap between cultural information and actual use.
